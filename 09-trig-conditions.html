What Are Trig Conditions?

By default, every trig you place on the Octatrack's sequencer fires on every loop. Step 1 plays, step 5 plays, step 9 plays — the same pattern, every time. Trig conditions change that.

A trig condition is a logical rule you attach to any trig. Instead of "always play," you tell the sequencer: "only play this trig if this condition is true." If the condition is false, the trig stays silent for that loop.

This is one of the Octatrack's most powerful sequencing features. With conditions, a single 16-step pattern can produce variation that sounds like it was programmed across dozens of patterns. You get:

  • Drum fills triggered on demand with [YES]
  • Probability-based ghost notes and random hits
  • Multi-loop evolution where patterns change over 2, 3, 4, or more bars
  • Chain reactions between steps and tracks

Conditions work on all trig types: sample trigs, note trigs, lock trigs, trigless trigs, and one-shot trigs. They live in the MICRO TIMING menu alongside micro timing offsets.

Manual reference: Section 12.13 — Conditional Locks (page 77). FILL mode details in Section 12.14 (page 78).

FILL & !FILL

FILL is the most hands-on trig condition. A trig set to FILL only fires when FILL mode is active. A trig set to !FILL (not-FILL) fires when FILL mode is not active. Together, they let you program two versions of the same beat: the "normal" version and the "fill" version.

How FILL Mode Works

There are three ways to activate FILL mode while the sequencer is running:

Action Button Combo Behavior
One-cycle fill Press [DOWN] + [PAGE] FILL activates on next loop, lasts exactly one cycle, then deactivates
Hold fill Hold [UP] + [PAGE] FILL stays active for as long as you hold the keys
Latch fill Press [UP] + [DOWN] + [PAGE] FILL locks on until you press [UP] + [PAGE] to unlatch

Performance tip: Holding [YES] while the pattern plays is the quickest way to trigger FILL mode in a live context. Many performers keep a thumb on [YES] and tap it rhythmically to create fills on the fly.

Setting a FILL Condition

1

Enter Grid Recording

Press [REC] to activate Grid Recording mode. The red LED lights up.

[REC]
2

Place your trig

Press a [TRIG] key (e.g. [TRIG 13]) to place a trig on that step. The LED turns red.

[TRIG 13]
3

Open the Micro Timing menu

Hold [TRIG 13] and press [LEFT] or [RIGHT] to open the Micro Timing menu. Keep holding the trig key.

[TRIG 13] hold + [LEFT] or [RIGHT]
4

Select the condition

While still holding [TRIG 13], turn the [LEVEL] encoder to scroll through conditions. Select FILL .

[LEVEL] turn → FILL

Remember: You must have [REC] active (Grid Recording mode) to access trig conditions. The condition menu will not open if you are not in Grid Recording.

Classic !FILL / FILL Pair

The power move is placing two trigs on the same step — one with !FILL (normal hi-hat) and one with FILL (crash cymbal sample lock). During normal playback, the hi-hat plays. When you activate FILL, the crash replaces it instantly.

To place two trigs on the same step with different conditions, place the first trig and set it to !FILL . Then on the same step, add a lock trig by holding [FUNC] and pressing the same [TRIG] key. Set the lock trig to FILL and assign a different sample via sample lock.

Probability (X%)

Probability conditions give each trig a random chance of firing on each loop. Set a trig to 50% and it fires roughly half the time. Set it to 25% and it fires about once every four loops. The roll happens fresh every time the sequencer passes that step.

Available probability values: 1% , 2% , 4% , 8% , 13% , 17% , 19% , 25% , 33% , 50% , 66% , 75% , 83% , 96% , 99% .

Setting a Probability Condition

The method is identical to setting FILL. While in [REC] mode, hold a [TRIG] key, press [LEFT] or [RIGHT] to open the Micro Timing menu, then turn the [LEVEL] encoder to select your percentage. The screen shows the value as you scroll.

[REC] → [TRIG] hold + [RIGHT] → [LEVEL] → 50%

Musical Uses

Probability Best For Example
50% Ghost notes, half-time feel Hi-hat offbeats that appear randomly
25% Rare accents, surprises Occasional snare flam or clap
75% Mostly-there hits Shaker that drops out sometimes
96% / 99% Humanization A kick that occasionally misses — feels live
1% / 4% Rare events Random FX hit once every ~25 loops

Pro tip: Layer multiple probability trigs on the same step using different percentages and different sample locks. A 75% closed hat and a 25% open hat on the same step creates natural-sounding hi-hat variation.

A:B Counting

A:B conditions create patterns that evolve over multiple loops. To set one, enter [REC] mode, hold a [TRIG] key, press [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to the condition parameter, and turn the [LEVEL] encoder to select the A:B value. The syntax is A:B where:

  • A = the loop number when the trig fires (counting from 1)
  • B = the total loop cycle before the count resets

The cycle repeats indefinitely. So 1:2 means "fire on loop 1, skip loop 2, fire on loop 3, skip loop 4" — effectively every other loop.

A:B Examples

Condition Fires On Loops Musical Effect
1:2 1, 3, 5, 7, 9... Every other loop (half-time feel)
2:2 2, 4, 6, 8, 10... Every other loop, offset by one
1:4 1, 5, 9, 13... Every 4th loop — 4-bar variation cycle
4:4 4, 8, 12, 16... Only on the 4th loop of every cycle
2:4 2, 6, 10, 14... Fire on loop 2, then every 4th loop after
1:3 1, 4, 7, 10... 3-loop cycle — combine with 1:4 for polyrhythmic evolution
4:7 4, 11, 18... Long cycle — trig fires every 7 loops on loop 4

Key insight: Combine 1:2 and 2:2 on the same step with different sample locks. On odd loops you hear sound A, on even loops you hear sound B. One step, two alternating sounds.

Polyrhythmic trick: Place a 1:3 trig and a 1:4 trig on different steps. The 3-loop and 4-loop cycles only align every 12 loops, creating a 12-bar evolving phrase from a single 1-bar pattern.

1ST, PRE & NEI

These three conditions create logic-driven sequencing: trigs that react to context rather than randomness.

1ST — First Loop Only

1ST fires the trig only on the very first time the pattern plays. After that first loop, it never fires again (until the pattern is restarted). Its inverse, !1ST , fires on every loop except the first.

Use case: A cymbal crash or intro sample that only plays when you first launch the pattern. Or use !1ST for a variation that kicks in after the intro loop.

PRE — Previous Step Logic

PRE is true if the most recently evaluated trig condition on the same track was true. !PRE is true when the previous condition was false. PRE and !PRE conditions themselves are ignored when evaluating the chain — only other condition types (FILL, X%, A:B, 1ST, NEI) count as "previous."

From the manual:

Example 1: [TRIG 1] = 50% is True → [TRIG 2] PRE = False → [TRIG 3] PRE = False → [TRIG 4] PRE = True

Example 2: [TRIG 1] = 50% is False → [TRIG 2] PRE = True → [TRIG 3] PRE = True → [TRIG 4] PRE = False

Wait — why does PRE flip? Because PRE looks at the most recent non-PRE condition. In example 1, [TRIG 1] was true (50% hit), so PRE on [TRIG 2] evaluates as true. But [TRIG 2] is itself a PRE, so [TRIG 3] still looks back at [TRIG 1]. The chain propagates from the last non-PRE trig.

Correction to the above: Actually, PRE evaluates based on the last evaluated trig condition result, but PRE/!PRE themselves are skipped in that evaluation chain. So PRE always references the last non-PRE condition on the track. This is subtle but important for building condition chains.

NEI — Neighbor Track Logic

NEI is true if the most recently evaluated trig condition on the neighbor track was true. The neighbor track is the track immediately before the current one. For example, Track 3's neighbor is Track 2. Track 4's neighbor is Track 3.

!NEI is true when the neighbor track's last condition was false. Like PRE, the NEI check ignores PRE and !PRE conditions on the neighbor track.

If no conditional trigs exist on the neighbor track, NEI evaluates as false.

Call-and-response: Put a 50% kick on Track 1, step 1. On Track 2, step 2, put a snare with NEI condition. The snare only fires if the kick hit — creating a "kick answers snare" pattern that is different every loop.

Every Condition Has an Inverse

Each condition type has a ! (NOT) version. When you scroll through conditions with the [LEVEL] encoder, you will see both the normal and inverted versions:

Condition Fires When Inverse Inverse Fires When
FILL FILL mode is active !FILL FILL mode is NOT active
PRE Previous condition was true !PRE Previous condition was false
NEI Neighbor track condition was true !NEI Neighbor track condition was false
1ST First loop of pattern !1ST Every loop EXCEPT the first

Combining Conditions with P-Locks

Trig conditions become truly powerful when combined with parameter locks. A conditional trig does not just decide whether a sound plays — it can change how it plays.

How It Works

After setting a trig condition (hold [TRIG], press [LEFT]/[RIGHT], turn [LEVEL]), you can still hold that same [TRIG] key and adjust any parameter page to p-lock values:

  • [SRC] — Change the sample (sample lock), pitch, timestretch settings
  • [AMP] — Change volume, pan, attack/release envelope
  • [FX1] — Change filter, EQ, or first effect slot
  • [FX2] — Change delay, reverb, or second effect slot
  • [AED] — Open Audio Editor for slice selection

Example: The Conditional Fill Hit

1

Set up the normal hit

On Track 1, step 13 (beat 4), place a trig with [TRIG 13] in [REC] mode. This plays your normal closed hi-hat.

2

Add a conditional lock trig

Hold [FUNC] + press [TRIG 13] to place a lock trig on the same step. Now hold [TRIG 13], press [RIGHT] to open conditions, turn [LEVEL] to select FILL .

[FUNC] + [TRIG 13] → hold [TRIG 13] + [RIGHT] → [LEVEL] → FILL
3

P-lock a different sample

Still holding [TRIG 13], press [SRC] to open the sample selector. Choose a crash cymbal. This sample lock only applies to this FILL trig.

[TRIG 13] hold + [SRC] → select crash
4

P-lock the filter

Still holding [TRIG 13], press [FX1] and adjust the filter cutoff. When FILL fires, the crash plays with a filtered sweep.

[TRIG 13] hold + [FX1] → adjust cutoff

Stack it all: One trig can have a condition + sample lock + filter p-lock + volume p-lock + micro timing offset. The condition determines if it plays, and the p-locks determine how it plays.

Practical Recipes

Recipe 1: Evolving Hi-Hat Pattern

Goal: A hi-hat line that sounds different every loop without you touching anything.

Setup: Track 3 with a closed hi-hat loaded via Flex machine.

  1. Enter [REC] mode. Place trigs on steps [TRIG 1], [TRIG 3], [TRIG 5], [TRIG 7], [TRIG 9], [TRIG 11], [TRIG 13], [TRIG 15] (all offbeats).
  2. Hold [TRIG 3], press [RIGHT], turn [LEVEL] to 50% . This hit fires half the time.
  3. Hold [TRIG 7], same process, set to 75% . This one fires 3 out of 4 times.
  4. Hold [TRIG 11], set to 50% .
  5. Hold [TRIG 15], set to 25% . A rare ghost note.
  6. Leave [TRIG 1], [TRIG 5], [TRIG 9], [TRIG 13] with no condition (100% — the backbone).
  7. Press [PLAY] and listen to the variation unfold.

Result: 4 guaranteed hits + 4 probability hits = infinite variation.

Recipe 2: 4-Bar Drum Fill

Goal: A fill that plays on the 4th bar, triggered by holding [YES].

Setup: Track 2 (snare) with a Flex machine loaded.

  1. Enter [REC] mode. Normal pattern: snare on [TRIG 5] and [TRIG 13] (beats 2 and 4).
  2. Set [TRIG 5] and [TRIG 13] to !FILL condition. These play when you are NOT filling.
  3. Now add fill trigs: place trigs on [TRIG 13], [TRIG 14], [TRIG 15], [TRIG 16].
  4. Set all four to FILL condition.
  5. Sample-lock [TRIG 14] to an open hi-hat, [TRIG 15] to a rim shot, [TRIG 16] to a crash.
  6. Press [PLAY]. Normal beat plays. Hold [YES] near bar 4 to trigger the fill.

Result: Hold [YES] = instant drum fill with sample variety. Release = back to normal.

Recipe 3: A:B Polyrhythm

Goal: A pattern that evolves over 12 loops from a single 1-bar pattern.

Setup: Track 1 (kick), Track 2 (perc), both with Flex machines.

  1. Track 1: Place kick on [TRIG 1] and [TRIG 9]. No conditions on these — always play.
  2. Track 1: Place a trig on [TRIG 5] with condition 1:3 . It fires every 3rd loop.
  3. Track 2: Place a percussion hit on [TRIG 7] with condition 1:4 . It fires every 4th loop.
  4. Track 2: Place another hit on [TRIG 3] with condition 2:3 . Different phase from Track 1's 1:3 .
  5. Press [PLAY] and let it loop. The 3-cycle and 4-cycle only align every 12 loops.

Result: 12-bar evolving phrase from a 1-bar pattern. Sounds composed, not random.

Recipe 4: Call-and-Response with PRE

Goal: A melodic or percussive phrase where step 2 only fires if step 1 fired.

Setup: One track with a Flex machine and a melodic sample loaded.

  1. Enter [REC] mode. Place a trig on [TRIG 1] with condition 50% .
  2. Place a trig on [TRIG 3] with condition PRE . This only fires if [TRIG 1] hit.
  3. Place a trig on [TRIG 5] with condition !PRE . This fires when [TRIG 1] did NOT hit.
  4. P-lock each step with a different pitch: hold [TRIG], press [SRC], adjust pitch.
  5. Press [PLAY]. On each loop you get either the "call" phrase ([TRIG 1] → [TRIG 3]) or the "response" phrase ([TRIG 5]).

Result: Two alternating musical phrases, randomly chosen each loop.
