1 What is Resampling?

Resampling is the act of recording the Octatrack's own output back into itself. Select a [TRACK] (e.g., [T8]), then configure its recorder to capture the MAIN output, [CUE] output, or any individual track's output into a recorder buffer — a special Flex sample slot that lives in RAM.

Every track on the OT has its own dedicated track recorder . These recorders are always available -- you do not need to assign them. They sit "on top" of each track and operate independently of whatever machine (Flex, Static, Thru) is running on that track. When a track recorder captures audio, it writes to its corresponding recorder buffer (Track 1's recorder writes to Buffer 1, Track 2's to Buffer 2, and so on).

Key Concept
Recorder buffers live in the Flex sample slot list , directly above the first sample slot. Once recorded, a buffer is instantly available to any Flex machine in any pattern in any bank -- project-wide.

Resampling is key for:

  • Building layers -- record your drums, then play new sounds on top
  • Creating transitions -- capture the current mix and crossfade to a new pattern
  • Mangling audio -- add FX, resample, slice, mangle further
  • Freeing up tracks -- bounce 4 tracks down to 1, then reuse those 4 tracks

2 SRC3 Setup

The heart of resampling is the RECORDING SETUP 1 menu, where you configure what the track recorder captures. The critical parameter is SRC3 -- the internal recording source.

Opening Recording Setup

  1. Select the track you want to use as your recorder by pressing the appropriate [TRACK] key (e.g., [T8] for Track 8).
  2. Press [FUNC] + [REC1] to open the RECORDING SETUP 1 menu. You will see parameters for INAB, INCD, SRC3, RLEN, TRIG, and LOOP.
  3. Set INAB to - (dash) to disable external input A/B recording.
  4. Set INCD to - (dash) to disable external input C/D recording. With both set to dash, the recorder will only capture internal audio.
  5. Set SRC3 to your desired source (see table below).
  6. Set RLEN to match your desired recording length (e.g., 16 steps = 1 bar at 4/4).
  7. Set TRIG to ONE -- this makes the recorder capture a fixed-length sample based on RLEN.

SRC3 Source Options

SRC3 Setting What It Captures Best Use
- (dash) Nothing (internal off) External-only sampling from inputs A/B or C/D
MAIN Full main output mix Capturing everything going to speakers
CUE CUE bus output only Targeted resampling of specific tracks
T1 - T8 Output of a single track Isolating one track (with its FX)
Common Mistake
If you leave INAB or INCD set to anything other than - while resampling internally, you will also capture whatever is coming in from external inputs. Always check all three source parameters: INAB , INCD , and SRC3 .
Pro Tip
Press [FUNC] + [REC2] to access the RECORDING SETUP 2 menu. Set FIN and FOUT to their minimum values (0.063) to apply a tiny fade at the start and end of your recording -- this prevents clicks when the recorded sample loops.

3 One-Shot Recorder Trigs

A recorder trig tells the sequencer to start recording at a specific step. Without one, nothing gets captured. There are two ways to initiate recording:

Method How When to Use
Manual Press [TRACK] + [REC3] Quick one-off captures, jamming
Recorder Trig Place trigs on the sequencer Automated, synced, repeatable captures

Placing Recorder Trigs

  1. Select your recording track by pressing the appropriate [TRACK] key.
  2. Open the RECORDING SETUP 1 menu: press [FUNC] + [REC1].
  3. Activate GRID RECORDING mode by pressing [REC]. The [REC] LED lights up.
  4. Press a [TRIG] key to place a recorder trig on that step. The [TRIG] LED turns red . While in RECORDING SETUP, the sequencer display only shows recorder trigs (not sample trigs).
  5. To make it a one-shot recorder trig, press [FUNC] + [TRIG] instead. The [TRIG] LED turns yellow , indicating it will fire only once and then disarm.
Critical: Why One-Shot Matters
A regular recorder trig fires every time the pattern loops. This means your carefully captured audio gets overwritten on the next pass. A one-shot recorder trig fires once and disarms -- your recording is safe until you deliberately re-arm it.

Re-arming: Press [YES] while in the RECORDING SETUP menu to re-arm all one-shot recorder trigs on that track. Press [STOP] + [STOP] while the sequencer is stopped to re-arm all one-shot trigs across the entire project.

Pro Tip
The recorder trig and the sample playback trig can be on the same step . When SRC3 is set to anything other than - , the sample trig will play back the previous recording while the recorder trig captures new audio. This is how the OT's simultaneous read/write works.

4 Capturing MAIN vs CUE

The choice between SRC3 = MAIN and SRC3 = CUE is one of the most important decisions in your resampling workflow.

Source Captures Best For Gotcha
MAIN Everything going to speakers (the full mix) Transitions, bouncing the entire mix Includes the recorder track's own playback if it has DIR > 0
CUE Only tracks routed to CUE output Targeted resampling, stems, submixes Must manually CUE each desired track
T1-T8 A single track's output (post-FX) Isolating one processed track Only one track at a time

Using CUE for Targeted Resampling

The CUE bus is like a separate mix that only goes to the headphone output. To route tracks to CUE:

  1. Press [CUE] + [T1] to route Track 1 to CUE. The [TRACK] LED changes to indicate CUE routing.
  2. Press [CUE] + [T2] to add Track 2 to the CUE mix. You can CUE as many tracks as you need.
  3. Now set your recorder track's SRC3 to CUE . The recorder will capture only the CUE-routed tracks, regardless of what is playing through the main speakers.
  4. Press [CUE] + [TRACK] again to un-CUE a track when finished.
From Merlin's Guide
"Set up a track recorder to record the cue output. Whatever combination of live inputs and tracks you need can be sent to it by cueing them, irrespective of what happens on the master outs. If you need a recording of the bass drum, hi-hats, claps, and input C/D, just cue these four, trigger the recorder the way you want, and you obtain a sample of what you selected."
Pro Tip
In the MIXER menu, enable CUE MUTES TRACK (found in PROJECT > CONTROL > PERSONALIZE). When checked, cueing a track automatically mutes it from the main output. This lets you silently resample tracks without the audience hearing them -- perfect for building transitions.

5 The CUE Transition Trick

This is the most famous Octatrack technique. It allows you to switch between completely different patterns with zero gap -- a smooth, DJ-style transition. The idea: capture the current mix, play it back, change everything underneath, then crossfade to the new pattern.

Step-by-Step Walkthrough

  1. Prepare Track 8 as your resample track: Assign a Flex machine to [T8]. In the sample slot list, assign the Recorder Buffer 8 (navigate above the first Flex slot using [UP]).
  2. Configure the recorder: Press [FUNC] + [REC1] on Track 8. Set INAB = - , INCD = - , SRC3 = MAIN , RLEN = 16 (one bar), TRIG = ONE . This captures the full mix for one bar.
  3. Place a one-shot recorder trig: With [REC] (Grid Recording) active, press [FUNC] + [TRIG 1] to place a one-shot recorder trig on step 1 (yellow LED).
  4. Exit RECORDING SETUP: Press [NO] to return to normal view.
  5. Place a sample trig for playback: In normal Grid Recording mode on Track 8, press [TRIG 1] to place a regular sample trig on step 1 (red LED). This will play back the recorder buffer.
  6. Let it record: Press [PLAY]. The one-shot recorder trig captures one bar of the current mix into Buffer 8. The sample trig immediately plays it back. After one loop, the recorder trig disarms -- the buffer is safe.
  7. Switch patterns: While Track 8 keeps looping the captured audio, press [PTN] + a [TRIG] key to queue a completely different pattern. When the new pattern starts, the audience still hears Track 8 playing the old mix.
  8. Crossfade: Set up [SCENE A] with Track 8 volume at maximum and all other tracks muted (XVOL = 0). Set [SCENE B] with Track 8 muted and all other tracks at full volume. Move the crossfader from A to B for a smooth transition from the old captured mix to the new live pattern.
CUE Transition Trick - Timeline
Feedback Warning
When SRC3 = MAIN and Track 8 is playing back the buffer through the main output, you risk a feedback loop if the recorder trig fires again. This is exactly why the one-shot trig is critical -- it fires once and stops. If you use a regular (repeating) recorder trig, you will record your own playback and get increasingly distorted audio.

6 Saving Recordings

Critical Warning: Volatile Buffers
Recorder buffers are stored in RAM only . They disappear completely when you power off the Octatrack. If you capture something you want to keep, you must save it to the CF card before powering down.

How to Save a Recording

  1. Select the track whose recording you want to save by pressing the appropriate [TRACK] key.
  2. Press [FUNC] + [REC3] to open the RECORDING EDIT menu.
  3. Select SAVE THIS RECORDING to save just the active track's buffer, or SAVE ALL RECORDINGS to save all 8 recorder buffers at once.
  4. The recording is saved as a WAV file to either the AUDIO POOL or the PROJECT DIRECTORY, depending on your SAVE SAMPLES TO setting (PROJECT > CONTROL > PERSONALIZE).
Pro Tip: Edit Before Saving
Select EDIT THIS RECORDING from the [FUNC] + [REC3] menu to open the recorder buffer in the [AED] (Audio Editor). Here you can trim, normalize, add slices, or set loop points before saving. This is especially useful if your recording has a bit of silence at the start.
From the Manual (p. 45)
"Pressing [FUNC] + [PLAY] while inside the RECORDING SETUP menus will clear the recorder buffer of the active track recorder from any audio content. GRID RECORDING mode needs to be inactive for this to function."

Once saved to the CF card, the recording becomes a permanent WAV file that you can load into any Flex or Static slot like any other sample. You can also copy it off the card via USB for use in your DAW.

7 Creative Techniques

Resampling is not just a utility -- it is one of the Octatrack's most powerful creative tools. Here are techniques that go beyond basic capture.

Resample with FX

Set up effects on your tracks before resampling. Since SRC3 captures the track's output post-FX , you can "print" effects permanently into samples:

  • Add a massive [FX1] reverb to a dry drum loop, resample it, then remove the reverb and slice the resampled audio
  • Use [FX2] delay with high feedback, resample the wash, and use it as a pad
  • Apply Lo-Fi (bit crush) to a clean vocal, resample, then switch [FX1] to a filter for further processing

Chain-Resampling

Resample a resample. Each pass adds more character:

  1. Record your initial pattern to Buffer 8 (Track 8 recorder, SRC3 = MAIN).
  2. Play back Buffer 8 on Track 8 with a Flex machine. Add [FX1] (e.g., filter with [LFO] modulation).
  3. Now set Track 7's recorder to SRC3 = T8 . Place a one-shot recorder trig on Track 7. This captures Track 8's processed output into Buffer 7.
  4. Play Buffer 7 on Track 7. Add different effects. Resample again to Track 6. Each generation adds texture and complexity.

Tempo Trick

Resample a pattern at one BPM, then change the project tempo. The recorded buffer plays at the original speed while everything else follows the new tempo. Use this for:

  • Gradual tempo ramps where the resampled layer drifts out of sync for a glitchy effect
  • Half-time/double-time juxtapositions
  • Capturing a fast pattern and playing it back in a slow, ambient context

Slice Your Resamples

After resampling, open the buffer in [AED] (Audio Editor), create a slice grid, and chop it up. Now you can rearrange the captured audio using [TRIG] keys in SLICES trig mode, or p-lock slice numbers per step for beat-juggling.

The Simultaneous Read/Write Trick

From Merlin's Guide: if you place both a recorder trig and a sample trig on the same step of the same track , with SRC3 set to - and the inputs active, you create a "live monitoring" mode. The Flex machine reads from the buffer while the recorder writes to it simultaneously. Remove the recorder trig and the last recording plays back. This turns the OT into a live input processor with instant capture.

Merlin's Tip: Abusing the CUE Outputs
Combine CUE routing with resampling for a "send effects" workflow. Route tracks to CUE, record the CUE bus, then play the recording back through a Flex machine with its own FX chain. This gives you effectively unlimited effects routing without using Neighbor machines.

Q Test Yourself

1. What does SRC3 = CUE capture?

A) The full main output mix going to speakers
B) Only external input audio
C) Only tracks routed to the CUE output bus
D) The output of a single specified track
Correct! SRC3 = CUE captures only the mix routed to the cue outputs. Route individual tracks to CUE using [CUE] + [TRACK] to build a custom submix for targeted resampling.
Not quite. SRC3 = CUE captures only the tracks you have routed to the CUE bus using [CUE] + [TRACK]. MAIN captures the full speaker mix, and T1-T8 captures individual tracks.

2. Why should you use a one-shot recorder trig instead of a regular recorder trig for resampling?

A) One-shot trigs record at higher quality
B) A regular trig fires every loop, overwriting your capture each time
C) One-shot trigs use less RAM
D) Regular trigs cannot record internal audio
Correct! A regular recorder trig fires on every pattern loop, overwriting the buffer contents. A one-shot recorder trig ([FUNC] + [TRIG]) fires once and disarms, preserving your captured audio.
Not quite. The key difference is that regular recorder trigs repeat every loop, overwriting the buffer. One-shot trigs fire once and disarm, protecting your recording.

3. What happens to recorder buffers when you power off the Octatrack?

A) They are automatically saved to the project folder
B) They are saved when you do SYNC TO CARD
C) They persist until you load a different project
D) They are lost -- recorder buffers are volatile RAM and disappear on power-off
Correct! Recorder buffers exist only in RAM and are completely erased on power-off. Always save important recordings using [FUNC] + [REC3] > SAVE THIS RECORDING before shutting down.
Recorder buffers are volatile -- they exist only in RAM and vanish on power-off. You must manually save them via [FUNC] + [REC3] > SAVE THIS RECORDING.

4. In the CUE transition trick, what is the correct sequence?

A) Record MAIN to buffer, play buffer, switch pattern, crossfade from buffer to new pattern
B) Switch pattern, record new pattern, crossfade to old pattern
C) CUE all tracks, record CUE, switch to new pattern, stop playback
D) Record the CUE output, mute all tracks, switch pattern, unmute
Correct! The CUE transition trick captures the current mix, plays it back on a dedicated track, switches to a completely new pattern underneath, then crossfades from the buffer to the new live pattern. Zero gap.
The CUE transition trick sequence is: (1) Record current MAIN to buffer, (2) Play the buffer, (3) Switch pattern while buffer keeps playing, (4) Crossfade from buffer to new pattern.
